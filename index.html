Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Why do you claim this impacts all $42.5 billion? I thought the reconciliation bill only adds $500 million to BEAD?

The legal trap is more devastating than it appears. While the bill appropriates only $500 million in new BEAD funding, the moratorium applies to any state that accepts any amount of BEAD funding on or after the bill's enactment.

Here's how the entire $42.5 billion becomes hostage:

  • All $42.45 billion from the 2021 Infrastructure Act has been "obligated" to states but not yet fully spent
  • If a state accepts even $1 of the new $500 million, the moratorium kicks in for that state
  • The Department of Commerce can then "deobligate" (take back) previously allocated funds for violations
  • Once funds are deobligated, states must comply with the moratorium to get any future BEAD money back

It's a legal mousetrap: states that initially refuse the $500 million can still lose billions in previously allocated funding through administrative action, then be forced to comply with the AI moratorium to restore those funds.

Q: Has Congress ever done anything like this before? What makes this different?

No—not at this scale or for this duration. Congress has sometimes preempted state rules in mature industries like telecom or aviation, but this is unprecedented:

What makes this a historic overreach:

  • It covers every use of AI, in every sector, across all 50 states
  • It creates a regulatory vacuum before Congress has passed any federal AI rules
  • It lasts a full decade—longer than any technology-related preemption in U.S. history
  • It uses infrastructure funding as leverage to strip states' constitutional police powers

Previous preemptions replaced state rules with federal oversight. This eliminates oversight entirely while emerging AI technology poses unprecedented risks to workers, children, and democracy itself. It's not coordination—it's a complete regulatory freeze that benefits only Big Tech monopolies.
